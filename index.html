Part One

The Paradigm Shift — From Technician to Diagnostic Leader

1.1 The Evolving Role of the Laboratory

In modern Kenyan healthcare (post-Devolution), the laboratory is no longer a passive support unit — it is the central nervous system of clinical decision-making. Over 70% of clinical diagnoses rely on laboratory results. This means that as a laboratory professional, your work directly determines patient outcomes at scale.

Your role extends far beyond running tests:

  • Laboratory Stewardship: Ensuring appropriate test utilization — preventing unnecessary costs and erroneous diagnoses caused by reflexive over-ordering.
  • Total Testing Process (TTP) Management: You are legally and ethically responsible for errors in the pre-analytical phase (patient misidentification), analytical phase (instrument errors), and post-analytical phase (critical value reporting failures).
  • Strategic Resource Allocation: Determining which equipment, reagents, and staffing mix optimizes patient care within budget.
  • Regulatory Stewardship: Ensuring the lab meets national licensing, accreditation, and environmental compliance standards.

1.2 The Kenyan Healthcare Laboratory Hierarchy

Medical laboratories in Kenya are classified according to the level of healthcare facility they serve. Graduates aiming for Class B and above must understand the full regulatory landscape.

To manage a lab legally in Kenya, you must navigate overlapping regulatory mandates. Compliance is not optional — unlicensed practice carries criminal penalties under the Medical Laboratory Technicians and Technologists Act.

📌 Critical Thinking — ISO 15189 vs. ISO 9001
Why is ISO 15189 preferred over ISO 9001 for medical labs? ISO 15189 specifically addresses the clinical competence of medical staff and the clinical utility of test results, not just general business quality management. A quality lab is not merely efficient — it must produce clinically meaningful data.

The Hierarchy of Biosafety Controls

Biosafety is not just about wearing gloves. It is applied risk engineering — a systematic, layered defense strategy. We apply controls in hierarchical order, from most to least effective.

1
Elimination / Substitution
Using non-infectious surrogates or fully automated systems to remove human handling of the hazard entirely.
Most Effective
2
Engineering Controls (Physical Barriers)
Biosafety Cabinets (BSCs), sealed centrifuge rotors, negative pressure rooms, HEPA filtration, directional airflow systems.
High
3
Administrative Controls
SOPs, restricted access zones, training programs, signage, work practice protocols, vaccination requirements.
Moderate
4
Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)
Gowns, gloves, N-95/P100 respirators, face shields. PPE is the last line of defense, not the primary safety strategy.
Least Effective

2.2 Biosafety Levels (BSL) — Classification & Kenya Context

BSL-1

Basic Laboratory

Non-pathogenic microorganisms. Standard microbiological practices. No special facility requirements. E.g., teaching labs using K-12 E. coli.

BSL-2

Most Clinical Labs

Moderate risk: Staphylococcus aureus, Salmonella, HIV blood samples. Requires Class II BSC for aerosol-generating procedures. All Kenyan district hospitals.

BSL-3

Referral / KEMRI Labs

Airborne Mycobacterium tuberculosis cultures. Double-door entry, anterooms, 100% exhaust HEPA. Five operational P3 facilities in Kenya (KEMRI-Wellcome, KEMRI-CDC).

BSL-4

Maximum Containment

Dangerous/exotic agents — Ebola, Marburg. Positive pressure suits or Class III gas-tight BSC. No operational BSL-4 in Kenya; samples sent regionally.

2.3 Deep Dive: Biological Safety Cabinets (BSCs)

In Kenyan Class B/C labs, the Class II Type A2 BSC is the standard primary containment device.

ClassPersonnel ProtectionProduct ProtectionEnvironment ProtectionApplication
Class I✅❌✅Low-moderate risk work, no product protection needed
Class II (A2)✅✅✅Standard for clinical labs — most common choice in Kenya
Class III✅ Maximum✅✅Gas-tight, BSL-4 work, glove-box configuration
⚠️ Critical Exam Point — Never use open flames in a BSC!
A Bunsen burner inside a Class II BSC disrupts the delicate laminar airflow, creating turbulent eddies that pull contaminants out of the cabinet and onto the user. Use electric micro-incinerators (e.g., Loop-L incinerators) instead. This is a frequently examined safety rule.

Part Three

The Science of Biorisk Assessment — The AMP Model

Risk assessment is the most legally scrutinized document in your laboratory. A poorly conducted risk assessment that leads to staff infection can result in criminal liability for the laboratory manager. The AMP (Assessment → Mitigation → Performance) Model is the international framework used in Kenya's national Biorisk Management Curriculum.

3.1 The Biorisk Equation

All risk decisions must be quantified using a standardized matrix. Risk is not a feeling — it is a calculated value:

The Risk Formula Risk Score = (Probability of Exposure) × (Severity of Consequence) Where: Probability: 1 = Rare | 2 = Unlikely | 3 = Possible | 4 = Likely | 5 = Almost Certain Severity: 1 = Negligible | 2 = Minor | 3 = Moderate | 4 = Major | 5 = Catastrophic Risk Level: Low (1–6) | Medium (7–14) | High (15–19) | Extreme (20–25)
🧪 Applied Example
Needle-stick during phlebotomy on an HIV-positive patient: Probability = 3 (Possible), Severity = 5 (Catastrophic — chronic infection). Risk Score = 15 → HIGH RISK. This demands immediate engineering controls (safety-engineered needles) and administrative controls (double-glove policy), not just PPE.

3.2 The AMP Model — Three Phases

A
Assessment — Identify and Evaluate All Risks
Consider: biological agent pathogenicity, infectious dose, route of transmission, laboratory activity type (aerosol-generating vs. closed), personnel competence and health status, facility engineering controls, and environmental factors including waste management. Use HAZOP (Hazard and Operability) analysis: systematically ask "What if X fails?" for every procedure and piece of equipment.
M
Mitigation — Implement Controls to Reduce Risk
Apply the Hierarchy of Controls (see Part 2). Mitigation must address root causes, not symptoms. For every identified risk, document: the specific control measure, who is responsible for implementation, the target risk score after mitigation, and the timeline for implementation. Risk below "Low" level is considered Acceptable Residual Risk.
P
Performance — Monitor and Review Effectiveness
Monitoring involves regular internal audits, incident review, QC data trending, and annual full risk assessment reviews. A risk assessment is a living document — it must be updated whenever new pathogens, procedures, or equipment are introduced, or after any laboratory incident.

3.3 The Swiss Cheese Model — Why Accidents Happen

The Swiss Cheese Model (Reason, 1990) explains why laboratory accidents occur even in facilities with many safety measures. Imagine each safety layer (SOP, PPE, engineering control, training) as a slice of Swiss cheese — each layer has holes (gaps/failures). An accident occurs only when the holes in all layers align simultaneously, allowing a hazard to pass through.

✅ Management Implication
Your job as a laboratory manager is to reduce the size and number of holes in each slice. No single control is perfect. You must stack multiple layers. When an incident occurs, the Swiss Cheese analysis reveals which holes aligned — directing your corrective action to close those specific gaps.

Part Four

Containment & Facility Design — HVAC Engineering

The facility itself is primary containment. Senior laboratory technologists and managers must be able to read and verify architectural and mechanical plans. Facility design errors are impossible to correct cheaply after construction.

4.1 Pressure Gradients — The Physics of Containment

In a BSL-2/3 laboratory, air must always flow inward — from clean corridor into the laboratory — never the reverse. This is achieved by maintaining the lab at negative pressure relative to adjacent spaces.

Pressure Differential Requirements BSL-2 Lab vs. Hallway: −12.5 Pa minimum (negative pressure) BSL-3 Lab vs. Anteroom: −25 Pa minimum BSL-3 Anteroom vs. Hallway: −12.5 Pa minimum Verification tool: Digital manometer — read DAILY by lab staff. Alarm action: If pressure alarm sounds → CEASE all aerosol work immediately.

4.2 The "Dirty-to-Clean" Workflow Design

Physical segregation of pathways is mandatory in all well-designed medical laboratories. Contamination travels only one direction:

ZoneFunctionAccess Control
Dirty Corridor / ZoneReceiving clinical specimens, disposing of contaminated waste, exiting soiled equipmentRestricted — lab personnel only
Laboratory CoreProcessing, testing, and analysisRestricted — trained personnel with PPE
Clean Corridor / ZoneIssuing reports, clean PPE storage, staff changing in/outSupervised access
Pass-Through AutoclaveDouble-ended unit set in wall between dirty lab and clean disposal area — materials enter dirty, exit sterileEngineering barrier

4.3 Key Design Parameters — At a Glance

  • Directional Airflow: Air flows from clean → contaminated areas; exhausted through HEPA filters (100% exhaust in BSL-3).
  • HEPA Filtration: High-Efficiency Particulate Air filters capture ≥99.97% of particles ≥0.3 µm. Annual certification required.
  • Physical Barriers: Solid walls, self-closing airtight doors, pass-through chambers with interlocking doors.
  • Waste Handling Systems: Decontamination before discharge — pass-through autoclaves, liquid effluent decontamination systems.
  • Emergency Systems: Backup generator (UPS), fire suppression compatible with lab contents, emergency eyewash and safety showers.
  • Surfaces: Non-porous, chemical-resistant benchtops (epoxy resin), seamless flooring with coved base (no crevices for contamination accumulation).

Part Five

Safe Laboratory Procedures — SOPs, GLP & PPE

5.1 Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) — The 5-S Framework

SOPs are not just bureaucratic documents — they are legal instruments and training tools. A poorly written SOP, or one not followed, creates liability. Use the 5-S Framework to ensure every SOP is complete:

ElementWhat it CoversExample Content
S — ScopeWhat test or procedure does this SOP describe?"This SOP covers malaria RDT testing for all samples received in the microbiology section."
S — StandardsWhat are the acceptance criteria (QC limits)?"Control line must appear within 20 minutes. Invalid results must be repeated."
S — SequenceStep-by-step instructions including what NOT to doNumbered steps with explicit prohibited actions highlighted.
S — SafetySpecific PPE and hazard warnings for this procedure"Minimum PPE: nitrile gloves, lab coat, and eye protection when handling blood samples."
S — StoragePost-test sample/reagent storage requirements"Dispose of used RDT cassettes in yellow infectious waste container within 2 hours."
⚠️ Document Control — Version Management
Every SOP must carry a version number (e.g., v3.0) and a review date. When a new version is issued, ALL old copies must be physically destroyed or stamped "OBSOLETE" in red. A staff member using an outdated HIV testing algorithm because the old SOP was left in a drawer is a serious patient safety failure — and a common accreditation finding.

5.2 Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) — Selection and Use

PPE selection must be hazard-specific. Generic gloves alone are insufficient. Train your team on the sequence: donning (putting on) before entering hazardous areas, and doffing (removing) in the correct sequence to prevent self-contamination when leaving.

PPE ItemProtects AgainstCritical Use Rule
Nitrile Gloves (double-glove for BSL-3)Biological and chemical hazards to handsChange between each patient; discard if torn or contaminated
Lab Coat / Fluid-Resistant GownSplash and contamination of personal clothingNever wear outside the laboratory; dedicated lab coat per section
Safety Goggles / Face ShieldSplash to eyes from blood, chemicals, aerosolsRequired for all centrifuge work and open-tube manipulation
Surgical Mask / N-95 RespiratorSurgical mask: splash. N-95: airborne particles ≥0.3 µm (TB work)N-95 requires fit-testing annually; not interchangeable with surgical mask
Closed-Toe ShoesDropped sharps and chemical spillsMandatory — sandals prohibited in all clinical laboratory sections

5.3 Good Laboratory Practices (GLP) — Core Prohibitions

  • No eating, drinking, smoking, or applying cosmetics in the laboratory (mouth contamination risk).
  • Hand washing with soap and water for ≥20 seconds before leaving the laboratory — hand sanitizer is a supplement, not a substitute when hands are visibly contaminated.
  • Decontaminate work surfaces at the start of the day, after each task, and at the end of each shift.
  • All containers must be clearly labeled with contents, concentration, and hazard classification.
  • Never pipette by mouth — use mechanical pipetting devices only.
  • Report all accidents, incidents, and near-misses immediately, regardless of perceived severity.

6.2 Sterilization Kinetics — The D-Value (University-Level)

For examinations, you must understand why specific sterilization parameters are set, not just what they are. Microbial death follows first-order kinetics — populations die at a constant rate relative to their size.

The D-Value (Decimal Reduction Time) D-Value = Time required to kill 90% (1 log₁₀ cycle) of a microbial population Clinical Application: If an autoclave cycle kills 1 log per minute at 121°C... Starting population of 10⁶ spores: → After 6 minutes: 10⁰ = 1 survivor → After 12 minutes: 10⁻⁶ = theoretical 0.000001 probability of a single survivor Standard autoclave cycles are set for at least 12 D-Values of reduction. This is called the "Sterility Assurance Level" (SAL) of 10⁻⁶.

Why use Geobacillus stearothermophilus spores as biological indicators? Because these spores have a very high D-value at 121°C — they are among the most heat-resistant organisms known. If the autoclave cycle kills these spores, you have mathematical certainty that all less-resistant organisms are also dead. A negative biological indicator (spore strip — no growth) is your highest-level sterility assurance.

7.2 Critical Chemical Incompatibilities — Never Mix These

🚫 Dangerous Chemical Combinations
  • HCl + Bleach (NaOCl): Produces highly toxic Chlorine gas (Cl₂) — can be lethal in confined spaces.
  • Cyanide + Acid: Produces Hydrogen Cyanide (HCN) gas — rapidly fatal.
  • Acids + Bases: Highly exothermic reaction; may cause violent boiling and spattering. Store separately.
  • Xylene + Strong Oxidizers: Fire and explosion hazard in histology processing areas.

Laboratory Resource Management — Supply Chain & Equipment

8.1 Inventory Management — ABC Analysis

Not all laboratory supplies deserve equal attention. The ABC Analysis (Pareto Principle applied to inventory) directs management effort proportionally to cost and criticality.

Category% of Budget% of Stock LinesReview FrequencyKenyan Examples
A — High Value/Critical~70-80%~10-20%Weekly / Continuous monitoringCD4 reagents, HIV rapid tests, haematology reagents, culture media
B — Medium Value~15-25%~30%MonthlyBiochemistry reagents, slides, tubes
C — Low Value/Bulk~5%~50-60%QuarterlyCotton wool, gauze, labels, specimen bags
Safety Stock Calculation (Critical for Kenya's Supply Chain Reality) Safety Stock = Average Daily Usage × Emergency Lead Time (days) Example: CD4 reagents Average Daily Usage = 20 tests/day Emergency Lead Time = 30 days (Mombasa port + customs + distribution) Safety Stock = 20 × 30 = 600 test minimum buffer stock Reorder Point = Safety Stock + (Average Daily Usage × Standard Lead Time)

8.2 Cold Chain Management — Kenya-Specific Considerations

Kenya's power infrastructure challenges (load shedding, voltage fluctuations) make cold chain management a critical clinical skill, not just an administrative task. Reagent failures due to temperature excursions are a leading cause of invalid results in resource-limited settings.

  • Data Loggers: All 2–8°C refrigerators must have continuous temperature data loggers. Review min/max graphs daily and sign the log. A forgotten temperature excursion is a liability.
  • Corrective Action Protocol: If refrigerator temperature exceeds 8°C: (1) Quarantine all reagents immediately, (2) Contact manufacturer for guidance, (3) Run QC on a known control material before resuming patient testing, (4) Document and report. Do NOT assume reagents are still valid.
  • UPS / Generator: Refrigerators storing critical reagents must be on uninterruptible power supply or generator backup. Include in your procurement justification.
  • Freeze-Thaw Cycles: Many reagents (especially monoclonal antibodies in immunohaematology) are destroyed by freeze-thaw cycling. Aliquot reagents that cannot withstand repeated freezing.

8.3 Equipment Management — The Full Lifecycle

1
Installation & Commissioning
Verify equipment against purchase specifications. Document serial numbers, software versions, and installation conditions. Never put equipment into clinical service without a commissioning check.
2
Calibration
Regular performance verification against traceable reference materials. Frequency per manufacturer's specification and QC data trends. Document calibration results and attach certificates.
3
Preventive Maintenance (PM)
Scheduled maintenance per manufacturer's checklist (daily, weekly, monthly, annual). PM extends equipment life and prevents unexpected downtime. Maintain a PM log for each piece of equipment.
4
Repair & Validation Post-Repair
After any repair, the equipment MUST be validated with QC material and reference samples before returning to patient testing. Never assume a repaired instrument performs correctly without verification.
5
Decommissioning
Decontaminate before disposal, removal, or service. Follow NEMA regulations for electronic waste. Remove from active equipment inventory and document decommissioning.

Part Nine

The Total Testing Cycle — Managing Preanalytical Errors

Research consistently shows that up to 70% of all laboratory errors occur in the pre-analytical phase — before a sample even reaches the analyser. As a manager, this is where your quality improvement efforts will have the greatest patient impact.

9.1 The Three Phases of the Testing Cycle

PhaseSteps InvolvedCommon Error Sources
Pre-Analytical (~70% of errors)Test ordering, patient identification, patient preparation, specimen collection, transport, receipt, processingWrong patient ID, incorrect order, wrong tube type, haemolysis, contamination, delayed transport, mislabeling
Analytical (~15% of errors)Test performance, QC assessment, result generationInstrument malfunction, reagent failure, QC not checked, interference effects
Post-Analytical (~15% of errors)Result verification, reporting, interpretation, data storageTranscription errors, reporting to wrong patient, critical value not communicated, misinterpretation

9.2 Phlebotomy — Critical Variables

⚠️ Order of Draw — CLSI Standards (Critical to Memorise)
The correct order of draw prevents carryover of additives between tubes:
  1. Blood Cultures (sterile technique first)
  2. Sodium Citrate (light blue — coagulation studies)
  3. Serum / SST (red / gold — biochemistry, serology)
  4. Lithium Heparin (green — plasma chemistry)
  5. EDTA (purple — haematology, full blood count)
  6. Fluoride-Oxalate (grey — glucose, lactate)
Exam pitfall: Drawing EDTA before Citrate causes EDTA's potassium-chelating action to contaminate the coagulation sample → falsely prolonged APTT. This is a reportable pre-analytical error.

Haemolysis Management: Free haemoglobin released from lysed red cells interferes with spectrophotometric assays (LDH, Potassium, Bilirubin). Advanced labs report a Haemolysis Index (HI) to clinicians alongside affected results. Implement a policy: "Results with HI >2+ must be reported with the comment: 'Result may be affected by in-vitro haemolysis; clinical correlation advised.'"

9.3 Laboratory Information Management System (LIMS)

A LIMS is the digital backbone of a modern laboratory. Under the Kenya Data Protection Act (2019), patient laboratory results are classified as sensitive personal data with specific handling requirements:

  • Audit Trails: The LIMS must log who accessed, modified, or reported each result — with timestamp and user ID. Unauthorized access to patient data is a criminal offence.
  • Critical Value Notifications: The LIMS must flag critical values (e.g., K⁺ > 6.5 mmol/L, Hb < 5 g/dL) for immediate telephonic communication to the clinician, with verbal read-back documented in the system.
  • Backup and Disaster Recovery: Data must be backed up at minimum daily, with off-site storage. Test the restoration process quarterly — a backup you cannot restore is not a backup.

Quality Management — Six Sigma, Westgard Rules & QMS

10.1 Sigma Metrics — The Modern QC Framework

The Sigma metric (σ) is a single number that tells you how well your analytical method performs relative to clinical requirements. It integrates both imprecision and inaccuracy into one quality score.

Part Eleven

Personnel Management — Just Culture & Competency

11.1 The Just Culture Model — Moving Beyond Blame

The outdated "name, blame, shame" approach to laboratory errors suppresses incident reporting and prevents systemic learning. The Just Culture Model distinguishes between three types of unsafe behaviour and responds appropriately to each:

Behaviour TypeDescriptionExampleManagement Response
Human ErrorInadvertent — "I didn't know / I forgot"Junior tech forgot to check patient ID wristbandConsole, retrain, and redesign the system to prevent recurrence (double-check protocol)
At-Risk BehaviourKnew the SOP but chose a shortcut — risk not appreciatedTech skipped QC because "the machine was working fine yesterday"Coach on the risk, increase supervision, and address workload root causes
Reckless BehaviourKnowingly and unjustifiably took a substantial riskFalsified QC records to avoid repeat runsDisciplinary action up to dismissal and KMLTTB licence review

11.2 Competency Assessment Programme

Competency assessment is mandatory for ISO 15189 compliance. It must be objective, documented, and cover both technical and safety skills. Assessment must occur at onboarding and then at least annually for each procedure the staff member performs.

  • Direct Observation of Routine Work: Watch the technologist perform the actual procedure (phlebotomy, Gram staining, operating the full blood count analyser) under normal working conditions.
  • Blind Proficiency Testing (Internal): Insert unknown specimens with known results into the daily workload. Record if the staff member gets the correct result without knowing it is a test sample.
  • Safety Drills (Surprise): Unannounced fire evacuation or spill response drill. Tests real-time knowledge, not memorized answers. Document who performed correctly and who did not.
  • Record Review: Assess the accuracy, completeness, and timeliness of the staff member's records and documentation over the assessment period.
  • Written/Practical Examination: For specific specialized procedures (e.g., TB culture, blood grouping), formal examinations provide additional validation of theoretical knowledge.

Part Thirteen

Transport of Infectious Substances — IATA DGR

As a laboratory manager, you may be a certified "Dangerous Goods Shipper" under IATA (International Air Transport Association) Dangerous Goods Regulations. Non-compliance can result in fines and removal from air transport programmes — critical for KEMRI and public health reference laboratory operations.

13.1 Classification — Category A vs. Category B

13.2 The Triple Packaging System — Category A

1
Primary Receptacle
Leak-proof, sealed specimen tube or container. Wrapped in sufficient absorbent material (e.g., paper towels) to absorb the entire contents if broken. Critical: the absorbent must be between the primary and secondary containers.
2
Secondary Packaging
Watertight, leak-proof bag or rigid secondary container. Multiple primary containers may be placed in one secondary container if they are individually wrapped. Include the specimen list inside this layer.
3
Outer Packaging
Rigid outer box (UN-certified). Must display: biohazard symbol, UN number, proper shipping name ("Infectious Substance, Affecting Humans"), shipper/receiver details, emergency contact, and quantity of dry ice (if used — must state net weight in kg, as sublimating CO₂ can rupture the package in cargo holds).

Part Fourteen

Emergency Preparedness & Occupational Health Surveillance

14.1 Spill Management — Advanced Protocol

For a high-risk spill (e.g., centrifuge rotor crack releasing a TB-positive sputum aerosol), standard wipe-and-clean is inadequate and dangerous. Follow this advanced protocol:

1
Evacuate & Close
Clear the room immediately. Close the door. Post a biohazard warning notice. Alert the laboratory manager.
2
Wait for Aerosol Settlement (≥30 minutes)
Airborne TB particles (droplet nuclei, 1–5 µm) remain suspended for up to 30 minutes. Re-entering before settlement negates all PPE effectiveness.
3
Full PPE Donning
Double-glove, N-95 (or PAPR — Powered Air-Purifying Respirator for high-risk exposures), impermeable apron or gown, eye protection. Have a buddy witness and assist with donning sequence.
4
Apply Disinfectant — Edges First
CRITICAL technique: pour 0.5–1% NaOCl around the edges of the spill first, working inward toward the center. This prevents splashing the agent outward. Never pour directly onto the center of a large spill.
5
Contact Time — 30 Minutes
Allow disinfectant to remain in contact for the full 30 minutes. Do not disturb. Add more disinfectant if the area dries out.
6
Mechanical Clean-up & Terminal Clean
Use an autoclavable scoop and pan to remove solids. Wipe with absorbent material. Follow with 70% ethanol wipe to remove corrosive bleach residue (especially on centrifuge rotor metal). All clean-up materials go into yellow infectious waste.
7
Doff PPE Correctly & Report
Remove PPE in correct order (gloves last). Wash hands. Complete an Incident Report within 24 hours. Initiate investigation.

14.2 Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP) for HIV — Manager's Protocol

🚨 Time-Critical — Maximum Effectiveness Window: 72 Hours
PEP must be started within 2 hours of exposure for maximum effectiveness. Effectiveness declines after 24 hours. After 72 hours, PEP is not indicated. The laboratory manager must ensure the PEP starter pack (TLD — Tenofovir/Lamivudine/Dolutegravir, per current Kenya ARV guidelines) is accessible 24/7.

⚠️ Fire Extinguisher Selection in Labs
Class B (CO₂ or Dry Powder) for flammable liquid fires. Never use water on electrical or chemical fires. CO₂ extinguishers are preferred in labs as they leave no residue on instruments. Familiarise yourself with extinguisher locations and types during orientation — before a fire occurs.

Part Fifteen

Incident Management — Reporting, Investigation & Root Cause Analysis

15.1 Types of Events to Report

  • Accidents: Unintended events that cause actual harm (needle-stick, chemical splash, patient misidentification with wrong results reported).
  • Incidents: Unintended events that caused no harm but had the potential to do so.
  • Near Misses: Events that almost caused harm but were caught by existing safety systems. These are the most valuable learning opportunities — they reveal where your defences are thin.
  • Sentinel Events: Serious patient harm or death resulting from a laboratory error — requires immediate notification to facility management and regulatory bodies.
📌 Culture of Reporting
A laboratory with zero incidents reported is not necessarily safe — it may simply have a culture of fear and under-reporting. A hallmark of a mature quality system is a high near-miss reporting rate coupled with low serious incident rate. Celebrate near-miss reports. They are gifts.

15.2 Root Cause Analysis Tools

Surface-level investigation ("the tech was careless") does not prevent recurrence. True root cause analysis (RCA) identifies the system failures that allowed the error to happen.

ToolMethodBest Used For
5 WhysAsk "why?" five times consecutively to drill from symptom to root causeSimple, single-cause incidents; rapid analysis
Fishbone (Ishikawa) DiagramMap causes across 6 M categories: Man, Machine, Material, Method, Measurement, Mother Nature (Environment)Complex, multi-factorial incidents; brainstorming team sessions
Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA)Proactively identify potential failure modes before an incident occurs; score by Severity × Occurrence × DetectabilityNew equipment/procedure implementation; high-risk process redesign
Swiss Cheese Model AnalysisMap which layers of defense failed and how they aligned to allow the incidentUnderstanding systemic failures in multi-layered safety systems

15.3 The CAPA Cycle — Corrective and Preventive Action

1
Identify the Non-Conformance
Document the specific deviation from policy, procedure, or expected quality outcome. Be precise and factual — avoid language like "careless" or "didn't try."
2
Contain Immediate Impact
Quarantine affected samples, results, or equipment. Alert clinicians if patient results may have been affected. Prevent further non-conforming output while the investigation is ongoing.
3
Conduct Root Cause Analysis
Use the appropriate RCA tool. Involve the front-line staff who were present — they have the most accurate account. Avoid premature conclusions.
4
Implement Corrective Action
Address the identified root cause — not just the symptom. Assign specific individuals, deadlines, and required resources for each action. Document in the CAPA register.
5
Implement Preventive Action
Consider: could this same failure happen elsewhere in the lab, or in a different process? Apply the learning laterally across the system.
6
Verify Effectiveness
After implementation, collect objective evidence that the corrective action worked. Did the error recur? Did the target indicator improve? Close the CAPA only when effectiveness is confirmed.
